\section{Experiments}\label{sec:Experiments}

This section presents the results of our experiments, demonstrating the flexibility of the SBM pipeline for top-down biodiversity modeling. Five models are evaluated: GLM, GAM, Random Forest, CatBoost, and FFNNs. The hyper-parameters listed in Table \ref{tab:best-hyperparameters} were used to train all models. The predictor variables are the 15 variables list in Table~\ref{tab:environmental-features}, extracted at two different spatial scales, for a total of 30  variables. The response variable is site-level species richness. The final dataset consists of \(1,363\) sites across the UK with species richness values that range from \(1\) to \(35\) during the year 2020. 

The experiments are designed to answer the three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How do different models perform compared to each other?
    \item How do models perform with different strategies of cross-validation, specifically random cross-validation vs. spatial cross-validation?
    \item How do models perform in data-limited scenarios?
\end{enumerate} 

The first experiment compares the performance of different models to each other under 10-fold random cross-validation. These results are presented in Section~\ref{subsec:research-question-1}. The second experiment compares the models' performance under 10-fold random cross-validation vs. 10-fold spatial cross-validation. Two of the models were chosen, GLM and CatBoost, as they represent one statistical and one machine learning approach. For each individual model, performance was compared across the two cross-validation strategies. These results are presented in Section~\ref{subsec:research-question-2}. The third experiment evaluates the performance of models' in data-limited scenarios, with different levels of data availability that ranged from \(100\%\) to \(10\%\) of the \(1,363\) sites. Once again GLM and CatBoost were chosen for this experiment and comparison was done across the different data-limited scenarios. These results are presented in Section~\ref{subsec:research-question-3}.

\subsection{Model class comparison}\label{subsec:research-question-1}
Research Question 1 compares how different models perform relative to one another. This is commonly done in the literature especially when proposing a new model for community-level biodiversity metric prediction \parencite{ngor2023, cai2023, baggstrom2025}. We ran experiments comparing the performance of the five different models on our final 2020 UKBMS dataset described at the end of Section~\ref{subsubsec:data}. The distribution of species richness across the 2020 UKBMS dataset is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:species-richness-distribution}, and the spatial distribution of the \(1,363\) sites is also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2020_sites_distribution}.

    \centering

        Images/species_richness_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of species richness across the \(1,363\) sites in the 2020 UKBMS dataset.}\label{fig:species-richness-distribution}
\end{figure}

Within the SBM pipeline, GLM, GAM, Random Forest, CatBoost, and FFNN were evaluated using 10-fold random cross-validation. The same seed was used for all experiments to ensure reproducibility. 
The distribution of predicted species richness compared to the distribution of observed species richness is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:model-species-richness-distributions}. All models except for CatBoost demonstrate a sharp peak around the mean. CatBoost, however, has a slightly less dominant peak which is also not present in the true distribution. All the models demostrate limited abilities in capturing the tails of the distribution, indicating weak performance at the extremes.

Figure~\ref{fig:model-calibration-plots} presents the observed species richness vs. predicted species richness scatter plots of all five models. All models show a positive trend between observed and predicted values, but the strength of the trend varies across models. CatBoost and Random Forest demonstrate the clearest positive trend compared to the other models. The GLM and GAM demonstrate the weakest. The FFNN demonstrates a positive trend that is somewhere between these two groups. Across all models predictions are most concentrated around intermediate richness values. Furthermore, all models have a hard time predicting high species richness values and low species richness values. This is consistent with the distribution of predicted species richness values in Figure~\ref{fig:model-species-richness-distributions} where tail ends of the distribution are not well captured.

The evaluation metrics used for comparison, described in Section~\ref{subsec:model_comparison} were calculated separately on the held-out test set for each of the 10 folds. Table~\ref{tab:random-cv-test-metric-tables} reports the mean, standard deviation, median, and interquartile range of these fold-level metric values across the test sets of the 10 folds. Because the values in Table~\ref{tab:smape-random-cv-test} are rounded to three decimal places, there is an apparent tie for the best median and standard deviation for SMAPE. This tie was resolved by comparing the unrounded values, which are detailed in the table's caption

Table~\ref{tab:random-cv-test-metric-tables} shows not one model is the best across all metrics. CatBoost has the highest \(R^2\) along with the lowest MAPE and RMSE, while Random Forest has the lowest SMAPE. Additionally, CatBoost has the best median across all four metrics. When looking at measures of dispersion, the FFNN has the smallest standard deviation for \(R^2\), MAPE, and RMSE while the GLM has the smallest standard deviation for SMAPE. CatBoost has the smallest interquartile range for SMAPE and MAPE, while the FFNN has the smallest \(R^2\) interquartile range, and the GAM has the smallest interquartile range for RMSE. CatBoost and Random Forest have the strongest mean and median performance, but across variability measures there is no clear best model.

In Figure~\ref{fig:model-all-metric-box-plots} box-plots of the \(R^2\), RMSE, MAPE, and SMAPE values are shown. Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-r2} displays CatBoost and Random Forest's higher median \(R^2\) values along with the FFNN's smaller interquartile range. In Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-rmse} the GAM model has the smallest interquartile range, but it is followed closely by the GLM, CatBoost, and FNN models. CatBoost and Random Forest once again have similar low median RMSE values. In Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-mape} the MAPE of all models is shown. All models perform very similarly in terms of  median and interquartile range. Based on the MAPE box-plots alone, there is no clear best model across.In Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-smape} the Random Forest barely has a higher median value compared to CatBoost, but the interquartile range of CatBoost is slightly smaller than the interquartile range of Random Forest. The FFNN also performs similary to the GLM and GAM which are all slightly worse than Random Forest and CatBoost.

Taken together, the \(R^2\), MAPE, SMAPE, and RMSE values across the random cross-validation folds in Figure~\ref{fig:model-all-metric-box-plots} show substantial overlap between model performance. The MAPE box-plots across all models are almost identical; whereas, SMAPE and RMSE show smaller median values for Random Forest and CatBoost compared to the other models. Furthermore, every models' box-plots overlap across all metrics. This would suggest that there is no single model that performs the best consistently. However, the numerical metrics and visual checks in Figures~\ref{fig:model-species-richness-distributions} and~\ref{fig:model-calibration-plots} suggest that the machine-learning models, particularly CatBoost and Random Forest, generally outperform the GLM and GAM in terms of \(R^2\), RMSE, and SMAPE, while all models perform similarly in terms of MAPE.

\subsection{Cross-validation strategy}\label{subsec:research-question-2}

This section addresses Research Question 2, which is concerned with how models perform internally across different cross-validation strategies. We conduct intra-model comparisons using 10-fold random cross-validation and 10-fold spatial cross-validation. Figure~\ref{fig:comparison-of-ukbms-data-distribution-across-cv-strategies} illustrates the fold assignments of the 2020 UKBMS dataset with each color representing a different fold. The spatial clustering fold assignments are evident in Figure~\ref{fig:spatial-cv-distribution} and the random fold assignment is evident in Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-distribution}. In this section we conduct comparisons of cross-validation strategies for two models, GLM and CatBoost, as GLM is a statistical model and CatBoost is a machine learning model. Each model' performace is compared to itself across the two different 10-fold cross-validation strategies. Because sampling sites are unevenly distributed across the spatial region of interest, 10-fold spatial cross-validation produced folds of unequal size. This can affect summarizes of performance across folds if fold size is not properly accounted for. To make the comparison more robust, we report non-weighted, weighted, and pooled calculations metric summaries. The unweighted summaries treat each fold equally, while the weighted summaries weight each fold by the number of observations in its test set divided by the total number of observations. The pooled calculation combines the held-out predictions from all test folds and calculates each metric once across the full set of cross-validated predictions, so that every observation is compared with its corresponding prediction.

\subsubsection{GLM cross-validation comparison}\label{subsubsection:glm-cv-comparison}
The GLM comparison first examines the distribution of observed species richness distribution and predicted species richness distribution for the test folds of both random and spatial 10-fold cross-validation. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:glm-predicted-vs-observed-distribution-across-cv-strategies}, they are similar if we just look at the mean and standard deviation. The mean and standard deviation for the random cross-validation strategy are \(18.54\) and \(3.49\) respectively, while the mean and standard deviation for the spatial cross-validation strategy are \(18.41\) and \(3.58\) respectively, while the true mean and standard deviation are \(18.54\) and \(6.10\) respectively. However, the distribution of the spatial cross-validation strategy appears to overlap with the true distribution more than the distribution of the random cross-validation strategy. The scatter plots of the observed vs. predicted species richness across the test folds for both random and spatial cross-validation strategies in Figure~\ref{fig:glm-observed-vs-predicted-across-cv-strategies} show a different pattern. The GLM performs better across the random cross-validation strategy compared to the spatial cross-validation strategy as the points demonstrate a more positive trend and are closer to the 1:1 line. Points in Figure\ref{fig:GLM-spatial-cv-observed-vs-predicted-plots} are not as close to the 1:1 line and for each observed species richness value there is a wider spread of predicted species richness values compared to the random cross-validation strategy in Figure~\ref{fig:GLM-random-cv-observed-vs-predicted-plots}.

Table~\ref{tab:glm-cv-strategy-comparison} presents the performance comparison across the calculated metrics along with a weighted mean, weighted standard deviation, and pooled metrics. We can see that the GLM performs better with the random cross-validation strategy compared to the spatial cross-validation strategy across all metrics. This is in line with previous studies that have measured cross-validation performance for different models \parencite{cai2023} and along with what was expected \parencite{roberts2017}. There are small differences present in the random cross-validation strategy for the weighted and unweighted metrics due to the number of sites not being perfectly divisible by 10, which impacted the size of test sets within each fold. 

A noteworthy results is that the mean \(R^2\) values are negative for the spatial cross-validation strategy except for the pooled mean which is weakly positive. Negative \(R^2\) values on the test folds implies that the model performs worse than a horizontal line fitted to the test data sets data. This can occurrence if the test set variance is low and extreme predictions far from the test mean would result in negative \(R^2\) values. Alternatively, the pooled \(R^2\) under spatial cross-validation, which corresponds to the full set of held-out preditions shown in Figure~\ref{fig:GLM-spatial-cv-observed-vs-predicted-plots}, demonstrates a weak positive \(R^2\) value of \(0.156\).

\subsection{Data-limited scenarios}\label{subsec:research-question-3}
This section addresses Research Question 3, bby evaluating how models perform across data-limited scenarios. This question is relavant because biodiversity datasets often do not satisfy data requirements, and data availability can strongly influence model performance \parencite{pichler2023}. Exploring model performance in data limited scenarios is therefore of interest to researchers as it can help determine which models are more robust to data limitations and can help guide model selection. The performance of GLM and CatBoost are compared again across different limited data scenarios. We train each model on total dataset sizes of \(100\%\) to \(10\%\) decreasing in increments of \(10\%\). To obtain these smaller datasets, we randomly sample a subset of the 2020 UKBMS dataset described in Section~\ref{subsubsec:data}. The intra-model evaluation is carried out across 10-fold random cross-validation for each dataset size.

\subsubsection{GLM data-limited scenarios comparison}\label{subsubsection:glm-data-limited-scenarios}

Figure~\ref{fig:glm-data-limited-scenarios-predicted-vs-observed-distribution} shows that the GLM broadly captures the distribution of observed species richness until we get down to the very low data scenarios around \(20\%\) and \(10\%\). In the lower data scenarios, there are noticeable gaps in the observed distribution that are not accounted for in the predicted distribution. Furthermore, Figure~\ref{fig:glm-data-limited-scenarios-predicted-vs-observed-plots} shows that the GLM captures the relationship between observed and predicted species richness across all dataset sizes until we get down to the very low data scenarios again around \(20\%\) and \(10\%\). Before these low data scenarios, a positive trend in the oberserved vs. predicted plots can be seen as the points are closer to the 1:1 line and increase as oberved species richness increases. However, in the \(20\%\) and \(10\%\) data scenarios, the points are more spread out across the range of observed species richness values and there is a larger deviation from the 1:1 line.
