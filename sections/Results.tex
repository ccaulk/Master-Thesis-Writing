\section{Experiments}\label{sec:Experiments}
In this section we present the results of our experiments, which are designed to answer the three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How do different models perform compared to each other?
    \item How do models perform with different strategies of cross-validation, specifically random cross-validation vs. spatial cross-validation?
    \item How do models perform in data-limited scenarios?
\end{enumerate} 
For each model class, GLM, GAM, Random Forest, CatBoost, and FFNNs, we used the hyper-parameters that are list in Table \ref{tab:best-hyperparameters}. Those models were trained and compared for the three different experiments that were conducted.
\subsection{Model class comparison}\label{sec:research-question-1}
Research question 1 is concerned with how different models perform against each other. This is commonly done in the literature especially when evaluating a new model \parencite{ngor2023, cai2023, baggstrom2025}. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the SBM pipeline we also ran experiments comparing the performance of different models on our final 2020 UKBMS described at the end of Section~\ref{subsubsec:data} along with the 30 predictor variables shown in Table~\ref{tab:environmental-features} to predict species richness across \(1,363\) sites. The distribution of species richness across the sites is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:species-richness-distribution}.

From the results in Table~\ref{tab:random-cv-test-metric-tables} we can see that not one model class is the best across all metrics. CatBoost performs the best for mean \(R^2\), MAPE,  and RMSE while Random Forest performs the best for mean SMAPE. Conncurently, CatBoost performs the best for median across all metrics. When looking at measures of dispersion, the best model for standard deviation is the FFNN for \(R^2\), MAPE, and RMSE while the best model for SMAPE standard deviation is the GLM. CatBoost performs the best for interquartile range for SMAPE and MAPE, while the FFNN performs the best for \(R^2\), and the GAM performs the best for RMSE when looking at interquartile range. 

Looking at a box-plot of the \(R^2\) values in Figure~\ref{fig:random-cv-r2} we can see that CAtBoost and Random Forest are comparable for mean and median \(R^2\) while the FFNN has a much smaller interquartile range compared to the other models. The GLM and GAM have lower mean and median \(R^2\) values compared to the other models. A model's \(R^2\) is a common model-comparison metric because it summarizes the proportion of variance in the data explained by the model and is widely used and interpretable \parencite{nakagawa2013}. However, it is also important to look at other metrics to provide additional insight.

The MAPE box-plots across all models look very similar; whereas, SMAPE and RSME show a decrease in values for Random Forest and CatBoost compared to the other models. Furthermore, every models' box-plot overlap across all metrics. This would suggest that there is no single model that performs the best consistently. This is in line with much of the literature that shows that there is no single best model for all problems \parencite{mondal2021, cai2023, baggstrom2025}

\subsection{Cross-validation strategy}\label{sec:research-question-2}
Here we compare the performance of the different models across the random cross-validation strategy and the spatial cross-validation strategy, with the implementation described in Section\ref{subsubsec:training_and_application}. We look at two models, GLM and CatBoost, as one is a statistical model and the other is a machine learning model. We evaluate each model individually and their performance across the two different cross-validation strategies. Evaluations of the other models are presented in Appendix \ref{appendix:appendix_b}. This highlights the gaps in model performance when spatial autocorrelation is present in the data and how different models are affected by this when we separate the data spatially \parencite{elith2009, wang2023}. All models in this section we trained on 10-fold cross-validation for both random and spatial cross-validation strategies.

\subsubsection{GLM}\label{subsubsection:glm-cv-comparison}

The mean and standard deviation for the random cross-validation strategy are \(18.54\) and \(3.49\) respectively, while the mean and standard deviation for the spatial cross-validation strategy are \(18.41\) and \(3.58\) respectively, which the true mean and standard deviation are \(18.54\) and \(6.10\) respectively.
